Why this is the right solution

The corpus is 14% Devanagari characters but 33% Devanagari bytes (each Hindi char = 3 UTF-8 bytes). The given byte tokenizer therefore shreds every Hindi character into 3 tokens, wasting a tiny model's capacity and its fixed 2000-step budget on byte fragments. The two decisive, independent levers were:

  • Training dynamics — the baseline was badly under-optimized. AdamW + a warmup→cosine LR schedule + gradient clipping + weight tying + proper scaled init did most of the work (−0.36 bpb) with no extra parameters.
  • Tokenizer — a corpus-trained byte-level BPE (vocab 2048, ~2.35 bytes/token) roughly halves sequence length, so each step sees ~2× more real text and the window spans ~2× more characters. It is lossless (decode(encode(x))==x) and falls back to raw bytes for any unseen UTF-8, so nothing breaks on the hidden Hindi-heavy test.

Because bpb is measured per byte, the tokenizer gain is genuine compression, not an accounting artifact. Run 4 shows an honest negative result: buying context by halving the batch increased gradient noise over the fixed step budget and lost — the fix was to keep the full batch.
